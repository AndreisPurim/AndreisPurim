# The Midsummer 2025 Memorandum
By A. Purim

*Context:* Initially, this came from an internal 10-page document called "Preliminary Assessment  of Baltic Intelligence and Cybersecurity Risks under a new US-Russia Rapprochement" that I wrote for BSF on March 22nd. Ideally, it considered a few talking points for a planned event in June 2025 in Riga. However, since things appear to change rapidly, I've decided to put up a public version in this markdown file. The 10-pages can be summarized as follows:

----


As of Midsummer 2025, the Baltic States must accept the following premises as operational realities:

**1. The United States must not be considered unreliable, but assumed absent.**  Strategic planning and threat modeling must proceed on the assumption of U.S. abandonment in the event of escalation or conflict.

**2. Since the early 2000s, the United States has collected extensive classified and semi-classified intelligence on the Baltic region via the Five Eyes network.**  This data is now potentially compromised due to internal political volatility and the collapse of secure compartmentalization within U.S. agencies.

**3. Allowing CIA, NSA, and other U.S. covert operations in the Baltics was a strategic error—then and now.**  Operations such as the CIA’s “Site Violet” in Lithuania should have been evaluated for long-term liability and adversary exploitation.

**4. The possibility that the U.S. may leak, mishandle, or be compelled to disclose sensitive Baltic information must be treated as a certainty.**  This includes both inadvertent exposure and intentional release under political pressure or ideological alignment with Russian interests.

**5. Reliance on U.S.-controlled geospatial and satellite infrastructure (e.g., GPS) for military or critical systems must end.**  Simultaneously, the Russian Federation is presumed to possess functional and independent alternatives (GLONASS, Liana, etc.). These facts shift the asymmetry of combat operations in the Baltic theatre and alter the regional balance of airpower.

**6. An anti-Baltic media narrative in the U.S. may begin at any moment,** likely seeded by Russian disinformation and amplified by sympathetic or opportunistic domestic actors.

**7. Support from Canada and the European Union can only be assumed in short-term windows (estimated at 12–24 months).**  Domestic political shifts, economic strain, and war fatigue across the West may reduce both overt and covert support.

**8. Reluctance to fully integrate intelligence and defense structures across the three Baltic states was a strategic failure.**  Joint counterintelligence, cyber defense, and HUMINT coordination must now shift from cooperation to federation. A coming error would be overreliance on EU mechanisms instead of building direct Baltic and Polish partnerships.
